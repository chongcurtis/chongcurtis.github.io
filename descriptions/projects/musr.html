Is Carpal Tunnel cramping your hands after a hard day's work? Do you still hope to swipe on dates without frustrating your fingers? Well, have no fear because my team and I used brainwave data from a Muse headband to auto-swipe for you on Tinder.

This hack works by reading the brain's EEG data via four nodes located near the left ear, left forehead, right forehead, and right ear. This data is fed through an FFT (Fast Fourier Transformation) to reduce its volatility data and to break it down into different wavelengths labelled Delta, Theta, Alpha, Beta, and Gamma. Near midnight, we consulted a neurotech engineer and determined that attraction at the most primitive level can be measured by extracting the Alpha value from the PSD (Power spectral density).

Next, we tested this theory on a subject (a friend) and received promising results.

One unforgettable moment was when we started to explore relationships between these brainwave values and the subject's preferences. Over the course of 90 seconds, we recorded the EEG data of samples that the subject deemed attractive. Then, the subject viewed samples that they deemed unattractive.

With the data we gathered from this experiment, we compared the ratios between the types of brainwaves for each node, the averages between nodes, and even the variances in our data. Later, we instantiated a calibration portion to determine a baseline to compare our brainwaves to. After some fiddling with the numbers, Alpha brainwaves that were below this calibration average are understood as the subject is attracted to a sample and Alpha brainwave readings above the average are understood as the subject does not deem the sample attractive.

Finally, we decided to disregard the readings for all brain waves except for Alpha waves because they seemed to be the most prominent indicator of attraction.

All in all, Musr was the inside joke my team and I wanted to make for over a year and finally putting fingers to a keyboard made the wait worthwhile.
